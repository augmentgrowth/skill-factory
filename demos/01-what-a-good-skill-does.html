03 Newsletter

BeforeNo skill

Subject: Why Most A/B Tests Fail (And How to Fix Yours)

Hi everyone,

In this issue, we're diving into a topic that might ruffle some feathers: why most A/B tests are basically theater.

I recently came across a fascinating conversation with Priya Nair, an experimentation lead, and she shared some eye-opening insights about why most A/B testing programs don't actually work.

The Problem: Underpowered Tests

According to Priya, around 80% of the A/B tests she's audited were underpowered. This means they didn't have enough traffic to detect the effect the team was hoping for, even if the effect was real.

The Solution: Three Best Practices

1. They calculate sample size before launching, not after.
2. They test bigger swings, because bigger effects are detectable with less traffic.
3. They're willing to kill tests that can't reach significance.

Key Takeaway

At the end of the day, you'll learn more from 8 real experiments a year than from 80 fake ones.

Thanks for reading, and I'll see you in the next issue!

Preview: Eighty percent are decoration. Here's the arithmetic behind why.

Ten days into the test, someone drops the dashboard in Slack. The new headline is up 12%. A few reactions, a quick "nice," and the variant goes live.

Nothing about that moment felt wrong. It also wasn't evidence.

The number that should bother you

Priya Nair audits other teams' experimentation programs. According to Nair, roughly 80% of the A/B tests she's reviewed were underpowered…

Underpowered is a statistics word, which is why it slides past everyone in the room. So here's what it means in practice.

Why the last one is the hard one

It isn't a statistics problem. It's a readout problem.

"We ran an experiment" plays better in a room than "we made a judgment call." So the experiment gets run, the noise gets collected, and the slide gets made.

But the underpowered test is the less scientific of the two options. A judgment call at least leaves the uncertainty visible. The test launders it.

Try this before your next launch

One question, answered in writing, before anyone builds a variant: how long would this need to run to detect the smallest lift I'd actually act on?

And about that ten-day readout — the variant might really have been a winner. That's the part that stings. You have no way of knowing.

Read the "before" again. It isn't wrong. Every fact is accurate, every number is right, the structure is clean. If you'd never seen the "after," you'd have shipped it.
