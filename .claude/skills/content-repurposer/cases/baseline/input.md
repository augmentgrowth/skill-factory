## Transcript excerpt — "The Growth Room" podcast, guest: Priya Nair, experimentation lead

> **Host:** You've said something that gets you in trouble at conferences — that most A/B tests are basically theater. What do you mean by that?
>
> **Priya:** Yeah, I stand by it. Most teams run tests that could never teach them anything, and then they celebrate the results anyway. The number I keep coming back to is that somewhere around 80% of the A/B tests I've audited were underpowered. That means the test didn't have enough traffic to detect the effect the team was hoping for, even if the effect was real. So you run it for two weeks, you see green, you ship it, and you have no idea whether you just made things better or worse.
>
> **Host:** Underpowered — walk me through what that actually looks like day to day.
>
> **Priya:** Sure. Say you've got a landing page converting at 3%, and a team wants to test a new headline. To reliably detect a jump from 3% to 3.3% — which would be a great result, that's a 10% relative lift — you need something like fifty thousand visitors per variation. Most teams testing that page get maybe eight thousand visitors a week, total. So they'd need three months. Nobody waits three months. They wait ten days, the dashboard shows the variant up 12%, they call it a win, and they move on.
>
> **Host:** And that 12% is noise.
>
> **Priya:** It's almost entirely noise. Early in a test, the numbers swing wildly. I've watched a variant be up 40% on day two and down 15% by day nine. If you stop the moment it looks good — which is what everyone does, it's called peeking — you're basically guaranteeing you ship random winners. The test isn't measuring the headline. It's measuring your patience.
>
> **Host:** So what separates the teams who get it right?
>
> **Priya:** Three things, and none of them are exciting. First, they calculate the sample size before they launch, not after. They know going in, "this test needs six weeks or we don't run it." Second, they test bigger swings. A headline tweak might move conversion half a percent. Changing the entire offer might move it twenty percent — and a twenty percent effect is detectable with way less traffic. Small tests need big samples; big tests need small samples. Most teams do the opposite of what the math wants. And third, they're willing to kill tests that can't reach significance. If you don't have the traffic, running the test anyway isn't rigor. It's a coin flip you're dressing up as data.
>
> **Host:** That last one feels like the hardest culturally.
>
> **Priya:** It's brutal. Because "we ran an experiment" sounds so much better in the readout than "we made a judgment call." But an underpowered test isn't more scientific than a judgment call — it's less, because at least a judgment call doesn't pretend to be evidence. My whole pitch to teams is: run a quarter of the tests, on things that actually matter, and give them the traffic and time they need. You'll learn more from eight real experiments a year than from eighty fake ones.
>
> **Host:** Eight real ones over eighty fake ones. I love that.
>
> **Priya:** It's the whole game. Volume of tests is a vanity metric. Decisions you can trust — that's the only output that matters.
